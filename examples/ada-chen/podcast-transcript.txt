The Builder's Mindset Podcast — Episode 147: Ada Chen

HOST: Welcome back to The Builder's Mindset. Today I'm thrilled to have Ada Chen, three-time founder and CEO of NeuralPath. Ada, thanks for being here.

ADA: Thanks for having me. Love the show, by the way. That episode with Priya about debugging culture? Chef's kiss. I made my whole leadership team listen to it.

HOST: Ha! That's amazing. So let's dive in. You've started three companies. What's the through-line?

ADA: Tools that give people superpowers. That's it. My first company was a code editor for kids — we wanted to make programming feel like a video game. Second was an accessibility platform — making the web usable for everyone, not just people with perfect vision and steady hands. And now NeuralPath, which is about making AI work for small businesses, not just the Googles and Metas of the world. The through-line is always: take something powerful and put it in the hands of people who don't have access.

HOST: Your first company didn't make it, though. What happened?

ADA: [sighs] We ran out of money three months before product-market fit. Classic. We had this beautiful product, kids loved it, teachers loved it — but we couldn't figure out the business model fast enough. Schools don't have budget, parents want free apps, and we burned through our seed round trying to be everything to everyone.

The real lesson, though? I blamed everyone else for about six months. The market, the investors, my CTO. Took me way too long to realize: the failure was mine. I was the CEO. The buck stops here. Once I accepted that, failure became a teacher instead of an enemy. Now when something breaks, my first question is always "What did I miss?" Not "Who screwed up?"

HOST: How do you handle uncertainty? Because starting a company is basically living in uncertainty.

ADA: We're old friends at this point. [laughs] Look, if you want certainty, go into accounting. Starting a company means making decisions with maybe 30% of the information you'd like. The key is: be decisive, but update constantly. I make fast decisions, but I also reverse them fast when new data comes in. No ego about it. The worst thing you can do is cling to a bad decision because admitting you were wrong feels uncomfortable. Get comfortable being wrong. It's a superpower.

HOST: What frustrates you most about the tech industry right now?

ADA: The short-termism. God, the short-termism. Everyone's optimizing for the next quarter, the next funding round. Meanwhile, we're building systems that'll be here in 20 years and we're not thinking about what that means. It's like... imagine building a bridge and only planning for this year's traffic. That's insane! And yet, that's basically what half of Silicon Valley does with AI.

HOST: Any advice for first-time founders listening?

ADA: Don't build what's trendy — build what keeps you up at night. If you're not obsessed with the problem, you'll quit when it gets hard. And it WILL get hard. Also: talk to users before you write a single line of code. I know, I know, everyone says this. But I still meet founders who've spent six months building something nobody asked for. That's not a startup, that's an expensive hobby. [laughs] Sorry, that was mean. But it's true!

HOST: Last question. What does success look like for you personally?

ADA: Honestly? It's not the exit or the revenue number. It's when someone sends me an email saying, "Your tool changed how I run my business." That's it. That's the whole game. We got one last week from a bakery owner in Ohio who automated her inventory with our platform. She said she got her Sundays back. Her SUNDAYS. That's worth more than any valuation.

HOST: Ada Chen, everybody. Thanks for being on the show.

ADA: This was fun. Tell Priya I said hi!
